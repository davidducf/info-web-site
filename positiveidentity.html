The Internet and Positive Development of Identity

In her book Life of The Screen, Sherry Turkle writes in-depth about Multi User Dungeons (MUDs) and how they help individuals explore aspects of their lives they never knew existed. MUDs are virtual platforms that allow users with similar interests to interact with one another in an almost game-like setting. Users create an avatar that can take on any desired characteristics, and players interact with objects and each other using text in real time. A modern day version of this would be something like a chat room or a multiplayer role playing game such as Second Life. Turkle equates the anonymity provided by the MUDs as the primary reason why people feel able to act out unexplored parts of their identities, a process that can be liberating and healing if used positively.

Anonymity - Positive Aspects

One example that Turkle uses to show the positive aspects of identity development using the Internet is a man named Gordon, who was a self-described unpopular and unattractive high school student. Gordon created multiple characters on different MUDs and each persona was used as a tool to develop character traits that he wished he had offline. Gordon would make a flirtatious or more outgoing character to test out what kind of interactions lead to successful online relationships. The successful actions of his online personas allowed Gordon to incorporate new actions into his offline life that helped him leave his comfort zone. In fact, Gordon actually ended up marrying somebody he met on one of his MUDs. Matthew was another MUD success story Turkle mentions. Matthew created a character that was the opposite of his deadbeat father, and allowed him to act as a mentor and role model to other MUD players. This action allowed Matthew to feel validated in his failed attempts to help and mentor others in real life. The important idea behind Gordon and Matthew's success is that as we experiment with personas online, we pick up certain things that may translate into real world changes to our offline identities in a positive way.

Benefits For Immigrants

When people emigrate from another country, they are often thrown into a chaotic and confusing environment that requires significant adjustment in order to feel both safe and accepted. In 2005, the interviews of 70 Russian immigrants to Israel were analyzed as part of a study on how the Internet helps immigrants develop an identity in their new country. Results showed that the Internet can be a useful tool for immigrants when developing an identity in their new country while simultaneously maintaining connections to their home country. When an immigrant arrives to a new country, it is important for them to become quickly assimilated into the culture. Using the Internet to accomplish cultural research allows immigrants to overcome any language barriers by accessing websites that are in their native language (Elias and Lemish 539). When language is not an issue, a person can learn more about culture and customs at a quicker pace so that their new identity can be developed in an accurate way. The Web also provides immigrants with a form of social empowerment by allowing them to easily keep up to date with all of the current events in their home country (Elias and Lemish 541). The study results also showed that most of the Russian immigrants used the Internet to practice socializing with local Israelis via chat rooms and message boards because they did not feel comfortable enough with their identity as an immigrant to attempt to make friends in person. This example illustrates the effectiveness of the Internet as an educational resource and a sort of safe zone for immigrants as they develop their new identity.

Potential For Disabled Individuals

Web based communications such as social networks and message boards also have potential benefits for intellectually disabled individuals or persons with learning disabilities. Unfortunately, people who have learning disabilities are often assigned a hindering label by people without similar conditions. These labels directly affect how so called "normal" individuals interact with the learning disabled, resulting in difficulties maintaining face-to-face relationships (Holmes and O'Loughlin 4). The anonymous nature of the Web allows people with disabilities to bypass any labels, giving them the freedom to express their true selves. Leveraging the anonymity of the Internet to express one's true self has the potential to let users experience sensations that are either physically not possible or believed to be impossible due to social restrictions placed on disabilities. This idea can be extended beyond people with learning disabilities to anyone who has self-confidence issues or a self-diagnosed character flaw.
